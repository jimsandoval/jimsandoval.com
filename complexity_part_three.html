Coupling and Cohesion, Part 3

In Part One of this series, I dissected why combinatorial complexity is often underestimated and often destroys productivity. In Part Two I presented soem ways to break things into pieces to limit interactions and build on a foundaiotn of loosely-coupled modules. In this third and final part, I'm going to present some ways to make sure that a groups of eparate modules each built by separate teams can evolve together in way that look slike they all designed as of one piece, that they work together cohesively as a unit.

Cohesion is the flip side of coupling. Once I havea lot of smallish isolated components, how do I bring them altogether into one big thing that plays?

Cohesion: Control Single-points of Reference

Once your environment is sufficiently de-coupled to allow teams to work in isolation with full confidence they can deliver new functionalty while also being able to significantly re-factor without breaking things, then Cohesion becomes the main challenge. How do you get many tens or hundreds or even thousands of individual things to come together in a way that when viewed as a whole makes sense?

In my experience, ensuring good cohesion is the easiest thing to implement from the start, one of the the hardest things to retro-fit into large and almost impossible to sell to managers and other developers.

One of the most instructive examples of a very large-scale architecture that exhbits excellent coupling and cohesion dynamics is the FIX protocol. FIX is well-known within financial services industry but viritually unheard of outside of it. It is the way finanical firms interact with themselve. Thousands of brokerages, hedge funds, software providers, institutions, and exchanges are all tied together via FIX to trade many trillions of dollars per day in as close to real-time as one can get.

FIX exists pretty much as a field dictionary and specification for messages - enumerated lists of fields that comprise either an instruction or a notification. Although there are software providers that implement FIX and make it easy to send and receive FIX messages, it is not software per se. Nor is there any hardware behind it. One does not send FIX messages to the FIX system to route to a FIX participant. FIX is purely a spec - a spec than enables different particpants to cooperate together to perform work on an almost un-imaginably large scale.
