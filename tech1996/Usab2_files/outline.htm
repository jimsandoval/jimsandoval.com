Information Theory
  • Pioneered by Claude Shannon at Bell Labs in the late 1940s while trying to use a single phone line for more than one call
  • Problem: Information tends towards entropy; during transfer you will lose information and gain noise
  • Solution: Redundancy. Send through multiple channels and use strict rules within a single channel
5
Redundancy as Rules
  • Restrictions on what is allowed make it easier to interpret what you get
  • Example: English Language
    • Word length only, based on Hamlet, act III:
      • LAQ NRY WERWL GUHAUX WEH M LTA U CK JS ZSFX MRD AQA XHJ BTGNSL J FHTU IKI ARMRW
    • Word length plus letter frequency
      • NCRDEERH HMFIOMRETW OVRCA OSRIE IEOBOTOGIM
    • Word length, plus two-letter frequency
      • ANED AVECA AMEREND TIN NF MEP FORT SESILORK TITIPOFELON HERIOSHIT MY ACT
    • Word length, plus three letter frequency
      • TO DEA NOW NAT TO BE WILL AND THEM BE DOES DOESORNS CALAWROUTOULD
    • Human languages have 75% redundancy

Mutliple Channels
  • Communication types:
    • Explicit: intended content
    • Involuntary: cues that undermine explicit
    • Back-Channel: cues from receiver
  • Current computers provide different means - sight, sound, touch, physical movement (kinesthetics) - that can be used effectively to build highly redundant explicit communication and add back-channels, too

Conceptual Models
  • Design Model:
    • The conceptual framework project members have about the system - its intentions and inner workings
  • System Image:
    • The system as it appears and behaves, the Look and Feel
  • User Model:
    • The users mental model of how the system works

Normans Observations:
  • Mental models are incomplete
  • Peoples abilities to run their models are severely limited
  • Mental models are unstable: people forget, especially after periods of not using the system
  • Mental models do not have firm boundaries: devices and operations are easy confused with one another
  • Mental models are unscientific: people maintain unnecessary  superstitious patterns because they cost little physical effort and save mental effort
  • Mental models are parsimonious: People will trade extra physical action for reduced mental complexity

Psychology of Personality
  • Jung: 4 pairs of types can combine into one of 16 profiles (refined by Meyers-Briggs)
11
Tognazzinis Observations:
  • General population is heavily skewed towards Sensories (80%)
  • Engineers and psychologists are among the most highly educated individuals ... and have the greatest resistance to intuitive cognition (Berne, 1958)
  • Software teams are skewed towards Introverts and Intuitives (twice western-world averages)
  • For effective user-interface design:
      • Extroverts are natural communicators
      • Intuitives are superb pattern-recognizers and highly imaginative
      • Thinking vs Feeling is relatively unimportant
      • Perception vs Judgement is relatively unimportant

Tuftes Observations
  • Bright, primary colors are very powerful: use sparingly over somewhat drap backgrounds that afford good contrast over subtle gradations.
  • Concentrate on Data/Ink ratio: visual weight should be given to information. Eliminate chart junk.

Process Components of Usability

16
Task Analysis
  • Field Analysis:
    • Users: What are their abilities? Personality types? Do they like computers? What else will they be doing besides using your system? Will they use the system all day every day? Once a month?
    • Environment: Hows the lighting? Desk space? Is it a big site with everybody doing the same thing? Single user?
    • Goals: What do they do? What do they want the new system to do?
  • Joint Application Design:
    • Hold sessions with representative users to talk through possible system designs
    • Give them prototypes to comment on as early as possible
17
Usability Testing
  • Observe people actually using the software to find out their user model
  • Can be done formally, expensively (videotape, one-way mirrors, etc.)
  • Can be done cheaply by developing good interviewing and observational skills
  • Be sure to include new users - new not only to the system, but to the job, too
18
Summary
  • Learn some human-factors and the design philosophy of your target platforms
  • Embrace participatory design
  • Pay attention to the user models that people build after using it awhile
  • Dont generalize from personal experience; just because you like a feature doesnt mean others will
